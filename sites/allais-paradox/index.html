Exercise 36.5 · Decision under risk

The Allais paradox & prospect theory

No utility function over money can rationalize the typical Allais preferences. But a value function with probability weighting can — here’s how, interactively.

Step 2 · Why expected utility breaks

Suppose any utility function U exists with U(0) = 0, U(1M) = u₁, U(2.5M) = u₂. Plugging in the typical preferences:

A ≻ B implies
u₁ > 0.89·u₁ + 0.10·u₂ ⟹ 0.11·u₁ > 0.10·u₂
D ≻ C implies
0.10·u₂ > 0.11·u₁

These can’t both hold. The same 89% chance of $1M sits on both sides of pair 1 and both sides of pair 2 — by EU’s independence axiom it should cancel out, leaving the same comparison. People treat the two situations as fundamentally different.

Step 3 · Prospect theory’s fix

Kahneman & Tversky (1979, 1992) replaced two pieces of the EU machinery:

Value function (concave for gains, with loss aversion λ for losses)
v(x) = xα if x ≥ 0 v(x) = −λ·(−x)β if x < 0
Probability weighting function (Tversky & Kahneman 1992)
w(p) = pγ / (pγ + (1−p)γ)1/γ

The 1992 paper’s median estimates: α = β ≈ 0.88, λ ≈ 2.25, γ ≈ 0.61 for gains. Small probabilities get overweighted, moderate-to-large probabilities get underweighted, and certainty (p = 1) keeps its full weight.

The certainty effect at work. Under EU, the 89% × $1M term cancels across both pairs. Under prospect theory, w(1) = 1 but w(0.99) < 1 — so collapsing a near-sure thing into an actual sure thing gives an outsized boost that doesn’t happen anywhere in pair 2. That’s why A beats B but D beats C.

How well does this approximate humans?

Prospect theory isn’t the final word — but it’s the closest single model we have to how people actually decide under risk. It captures four robust effects EU misses:

1 · Certainty effect
Sure things are valued disproportionately more than near-sure things. Resolves Allais.
2 · Reflection effect
People are risk-averse for gains but risk-seeking for losses (β makes the loss curve concave too).
3 · Loss aversion
Losing $100 hurts roughly 2× as much as gaining $100 helps. λ ≈ 2.25.
4 · Probability distortion
Lottery tickets and insurance both sell because tiny probabilities get overweighted.

Cumulative prospect theory (1992) generalizes this further by applying the weighting function to cumulative rather than individual probabilities, which fixes some technical issues with stochastic dominance. For most descriptive purposes, the version above is what gets used.
