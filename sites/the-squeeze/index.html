Monthly income · USD equivalent

$50 A MONTH

Ethiopia has no statutory minimum wage. The median worker earns about $50 a month.¹⁰ 63% of the workforce is in agriculture — most as smallholders, two-thirds below the poverty line.¹¹ The chart describes a thin formal slice.

06 / WHAT CAME BEFORE
November 2020 – November 2022 · Tigray, Amhara, Afar

EVERY SHOCK ABOVE
STARTED HERE.

For two years before the float, before Hormuz, before the aid collapse, Ethiopia fought a civil war in its north. The Tigray War killed up to one million people,³⁴ destroyed roughly a quarter of Ethiopia's GDP in physical damage,³⁵ and broke the state's ability to pay its own way. Everything that has happened since happened on top of this.

DAMAGE & LOSS
$28.7B
Infrastructure damage + productivity loss in 13 months. 26% of GDP.

The Ethiopian government's own assessment, conducted with the World Bank, put physical damage at $22.7 billion with another $6 billion in lost productivity — for one year, in three regions. Reconstruction needs were estimated at $20 billion, roughly 130% of the annual government budget.³⁶ The country has not paid this back. Most of it never will be.

A government collecting 7% of GDP in taxes cannot rebuild $20 billion of destroyed roads, schools, and hospitals. It cannot subsidize fuel through a float. It cannot replace a departing donor. The state's fiscal capacity collapsed during the war and has not recovered. That is why every shock that followed — the float, Hormuz, USAID — cut so deep. Ethiopia entered the storms with no buffer.

Oxford's Initiative on Peace and Recovery in Ethiopia projects cumulative economic losses from the conflicts will reach $125 billion by 2027 — leaving the economy 19% smaller than it would have been on the pre-war trajectory.⁴¹ And the fighting has not fully stopped — Amhara, Oromia, and other regions remain in active or simmering conflict.

09 / THE LAST SHOCK
January 2025 · Trump executive order suspends foreign aid

THEN THE LARGEST DONOR
WALKED AWAY.

Ethiopia was the biggest recipient of US aid in sub-Saharan Africa — $1.8 billion in FY2023, $1.33 billion in FY2024.²⁶ On January 20, 2025, the Trump executive order paused all foreign aid for a 90-day review. USAID was effectively dismantled. Most programs never restarted.²⁷ The squeeze had a third shock waiting.

USAID's exit hits a country whose macro reform program was built on the assumption of continued external assistance.³² The IMF flagged the risk in its 2026 review. Other Western donors are also cutting — UK, France, Germany, Netherlands all reduced aid budgets in 2024–25.³³ The OECD forecasts a 16–28% drop in development aid to sub-Saharan Africa in 2025 alone.

For Ethiopia, this means the float, the fuel shock, and the aid collapse arrived in eighteen months — three structural breaks at once. The macro reform was supposed to wean the country off aid gradually. The wean became an amputation.

11 / CASCADE

THREE CHANNELS.
ALL OPEN.

i
Direct hit

Kerosene doubles. Cooking fuel for the rural poor and off-grid urban households. A regressive tax on people already living on a few dollars a day.

ii
Transport into food

Diesel runs the trucks moving grain to market, the buses, the irrigation pumps. Food inflation hit 13.5% in April. The pass-through is still working.²⁵

iii
Fertilizer

The Gulf produces nearly half the world's urea. Prices up 50% in the first weeks of the crisis. Lower yields or higher food later in 2026 — likely both.

13 / WHAT THIS MISSES

A PARTIAL PICTURE.

An honest accounting of the gaps

This piece tells one story — the squeeze on formal-sector wages, the currency, and fuel — and it tells it with city-centric data. That's not the whole picture, and pretending otherwise would be dishonest. The bullets below name what's underweighted or absent, and why it matters.

— REMITTANCES
The Ethiopian diaspora sends home $5–7 billion a year, more than coffee export earnings. Many "low-wage" households are subsidized from abroad. Without that flow, the squeeze would look far worse.
— THE INFORMAL SECTOR
Roughly 54% of employment is informal. Salary surveys don't see it. The "median wage" of $50 mostly captures the formal slice — the informal economy runs on day rates, kind, and barter.
— ONGOING CONFLICTS
The Tigray War gets a section here, but Amhara's Fano insurgency (2023–present), the Oromia OLA conflict, and ethnic violence in Benishangul-Gumuz are touched only in the map. Documented drone strikes, mass displacement, and continuing harm don't get the space they deserve.
— CLIMATE STRESS
The 2020–23 Horn of Africa drought decimated pastoralist livelihoods in Somali, Afar, southern Oromia. Fertilizer shocks amplify a longer climate stress on yields the figures here barely touch.
— SUBSISTENCE AGRICULTURE
63% of the workforce farms small plots, mostly outside the cash economy. Their lives don't move with the kerosene price the same way Addis lives do — but fertilizer, fuel, and food-import dynamics still cascade in.
— DATA UNCERTAINTY
Wage estimates vary 2–4× across sources. Median $50, average $190 — both real, both for different things. Numbers are presented with hover citations; treat ranges as honest, not precise.
