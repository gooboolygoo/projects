Ethiopia economy snapshot / May 2026

Ethiopia's cost-of-living shock

A fast-growing economy can still feel like a crisis at street level. This page explains how low wages, a weaker birr, rising living costs, and the Strait of Hormuz oil shock are converging on Ethiopian households.

01 / Wages

The first pressure point is pay.

For many U.S. readers, the hardest part to grasp is the size of the monthly wage base. A salary that sounds small in dollars is not a side detail. It determines how quickly a fuel or food shock becomes a household emergency.

Formal wage data varies by source and often misses smallholder farmers and informal workers. But the ladder below gives the shape of the problem: many essential jobs sit below, or only slightly above, the cost of a very basic urban life.

Why doctors matter

Health workers became a public symbol of the squeeze in 2025: years of training, very low state pay after the currency shock, and a strike met with arrests and only a tiny raise. It is not a separate story. It is the wage crisis in its sharpest form.

02 / Prices

Cost-of-living numbers describe two different Ethiopias.

International cost indexes often measure a middle-class or expat basket: private rent, imported groceries, internet, ride-hailing, restaurants. Many Ethiopians live on far less by sharing housing, cooking at home, using minibuses, and staying out of imported-goods markets.

The squeeze appears when even modest costs start moving out of reach: cooking fuel, transport, grain, rent, medicine, and school-related expenses.

03 / Shocks

Three shocks are landing on the same household budget.

The current pressure is not caused by one event. Ethiopia entered 2026 after a major exchange-rate reform and a fuel-subsidy phaseout. Then the Strait of Hormuz crisis raised the global price of the imported fuel Ethiopia depends on.

July 2024
The birr is floated

The official exchange rate moves from about 57 birr per dollar toward market levels. Imported fuel, fertilizer, medicine, wheat, and machinery become much more expensive in local currency.

2025
Subsidies are reduced

The government continues fuel-price reforms linked to the IMF program. Lower subsidies improve public finances, but they also expose households to more of the global fuel price.

Feb-May 2026
The Strait of Hormuz shock hits

A closure and military conflict around a key oil chokepoint push Brent crude above $120 per barrel. Ethiopia imports nearly all petroleum, so the shock arrives through fuel costs, freight, food, and the subsidy bill.

April 2026
Inflation returns to double digits

After easing into single digits, headline inflation rebounds to 11.7%. Fuel shortages and transport costs make the disinflation story fragile.

04 / Spread

An oil shock becomes a household shock through everyday routes.

Fuel is not just what a driver buys at the pump. It is embedded in bus fares, food transport, fertilizer, electricity backup, construction materials, and imported goods. That is why a disruption far from Ethiopia can show up as a market price in Addis Ababa or a cooking-fuel bill in a rural home.

Channel 1

Cooking and lighting

Kerosene price jumps hit poorer and off-grid households directly because the fuel is used at home, not only in vehicles.

Channel 2

Food transport

Diesel runs trucks, buses, pumps, and tractors. Higher diesel costs can travel into grain, vegetables, and commuting.

Channel 3

Fertilizer and imports

Energy shocks raise the cost of fertilizer and imported inputs, increasing the risk of higher food prices later in the year.

05 / Meaning

The macro story and the household story are moving in different directions.

Ethiopia can have strong GDP growth, better export receipts, IMF support, and rebuilding reserves while many families experience the reform period as a worsening daily squeeze. Both can be true at the same time.

The practical test is not only whether the national program is technically on track. It is whether wages, fuel, food, rent, and medicine stop moving faster than households can adapt.

The clearest way to understand the crisis is this: stabilization may be visible in macroeconomic charts before it is visible in a family's fuel bill.
